# Nature Exploration Media

Five connected, people-free scenes for the portfolio. The visual story moves from the
trailhead to the forest, through the mountain route and basecamp, and ends at a summit.
Generate the stills first; optional image-to-video loops can be made later from the same
stills.

## Shared prompt block

Append this block to every prompt:

> Photorealistic cinematic editorial landscape, realistic natural materials, restrained
> outdoor color grade, warm cream highlights, forest green, moss, slate, sky blue, and muted
> sunset orange. No people anywhere: no bodies, faces, hands, silhouettes, human shadows,
> reflections, portraits, mannequins, or visible hikers. No readable text, logos, branded
> signage, watermarks, or artificial typography. Keep the camera locked and the composition
> physically plausible, with no motion blur or surreal objects. Place the main focal region
> inside the central 70% of the frame so the image can crop safely on mobile. Leave calm
> negative space near the upper third for optional section copy. Create a 4:5 portrait base
> image; if the tool supports it, also make a 16:9 crop using the same composition.

## 1. Hero — trailhead at first light

> Create a photorealistic cinematic scene at a quiet mountain trailhead just after sunrise.
> A narrow dirt trail begins in the lower center of the frame and leads toward a broad
> mountain overlook. Show layered blue-green ridges fading into pale morning haze, a few
> granite rocks and low native grasses in the foreground, and one simple unbranded trail
> marker with no writing. Use soft golden light breaking through cool blue morning air.
>
> The image should feel like the first step of an open-ended journey: calm, spacious,
> curious, and inviting. Avoid dramatic wilderness clichés, huge impossible mountains, or
> luxury travel photography. Make the trail readable at a small mobile size and keep the
> brightest focal area around the trail and distant ridge.

## 2. About — the forest path

> Create a photorealistic cinematic scene on a shaded Appalachian hardwood forest trail
> after light rain. The path curves gently from the lower left toward the center of the frame,
> bordered by moss-covered stones, ferns, rhododendron, damp leaves, and dark textured tree
> trunks. A shallow clear creek crosses the trail in the middle distance over a few flat
> stepping stones.
>
> Use soft filtered sunlight through the canopy, small water reflections, subtle mist close
> to the creek, and realistic wet surfaces. The mood should be observant and thoughtful,
> like slowing down to notice details. Keep the path and creek as the primary visual route;
> do not make the forest dense, frightening, or fantasy-like.

## 3. Experience — the switchback

> Create a photorealistic cinematic view of a rocky mountain switchback trail in clear
> afternoon light. Show the trail entering from the lower right, bending across the frame,
> and continuing upward toward a distant ridge. Include exposed gray rock, weathered roots,
> mountain laurel, small grasses, and a wide view of layered ridgelines beyond the bend.
>
> Place a simple unbranded metal trail sign or trail post near the bend with no readable
> lettering. Add realistic dust and worn footprints only as natural trail texture, but do
> not show any person. The composition should communicate progress, effort, and discovery
> without looking extreme or dangerous. Use a strong diagonal route that remains legible in
> both a desktop crop and a narrow mobile crop.

## 4. Work — basecamp field table

> Create a photorealistic cinematic basecamp scene inside a simple mountain shelter or open
> trail cabin. Center a sturdy weathered worktable beside a window overlooking blue mountain
> ridges. On the table, arrange a folded topographic map with no readable labels, a brass
> compass, a blank field notebook, a mechanical pencil, a compact camera, a headlamp, a
> reusable metal water bottle, and a practical olive canvas backpack resting beside the table.
>
> The objects should look used and functional rather than luxury travel props. Use warm
> late-afternoon light across the tabletop, cool mountain light through the window, visible
> paper texture, worn canvas, brushed metal, and natural wood grain. The composition should
> suggest planning, research, making, and preparing for the next route. Keep the map, compass,
> and notebook in the central safe area, with no legible writing or brands.

## 5. Contact — summit at sunset

> Create a photorealistic cinematic scene at a quiet mountain summit during late sunset.
> Show a simple weathered lookout bench or flat granite ledge in the lower third, with an
> olive canvas backpack, closed metal water bottle, and folded blank topographic map placed
> naturally beside it. A narrow trail continues from the foreground toward a distant ridge
> and disappears over the horizon.
>
> Use a layered sunset sky transitioning from muted peach near the horizon to deep blue above,
> with soft warm light catching the rock and grass. The scene should feel peaceful and open,
> like the end of one route and the beginning of another. Avoid a dramatic expedition mood;
> keep it approachable, grounded, and quietly optimistic. Preserve clear negative space in
> the sky and keep the trail, bench, and gear readable on mobile.

## Media and responsive constraints

- Keep the composition’s focal area inside the central 70%; do not place essential detail at
  the extreme left or right edges.
- Generate a 4:5 base image for stacked mobile layouts and a matching 16:9 crop for wide
  desktop compositions when possible.
- Use a still image as the default and poster. Any future loop should use only subtle ambient
  movement: mist, light, water reflections, grasses, steam, or clouds.
- Do not use camera pans, zooms, parallax, people entering the frame, or objects that move
  across the focal subject.
- Export web-ready images as WebP or AVIF with a JPEG fallback. Keep optional video loops
  muted, short, and below 2 MB each.
